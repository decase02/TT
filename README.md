## Law Library Notice

This README is the legal library of the Central American Trading Company.

It is not merely a project description. It is intended to serve as the living index and working text for the Company's Charter Code, legal definitions, organic rules, statutory Acts, local charters, merchant regulations, treasury rules, token rules, compliance instruments, and future ratified legal instruments.

The Central American Trading Company is designed as a private, non-sovereign, host-law-subordinate governance-services company. It does not claim statehood, independent sovereignty, diplomatic status, military authority, criminal jurisdiction, public taxation authority, or the power to override host-state law.

The Company exists to provide lawful, voluntary, contract-based governance services to private communities, special jurisdictions, economic zones, charter settlements, digital communities, commercial enclaves, ports, logistics facilities, research zones, and private infrastructure territories.

The Company is inspired by historic chartered trading companies only in the sense of combining commerce, infrastructure, administration, logistics, law, and governance services. It expressly rejects conquest, forced rule, extraction, slavery, coercive monopolies, private warfare, involuntary subjection, and any claim of independent sovereignty.

# PART II — PRÓSPERA-INFORMED LEGAL ARCHITECTURE

## 4. Purpose of the Próspera Reference Layer

Próspera ZEDE is used in this Law Library as a reference model for lawful, contract-based, special-jurisdiction governance design.

The Company does not claim to be Próspera ZEDE, a Honduran ZEDE, a sovereign state, or a public government. The Próspera reference layer is used only to study and adapt useful institutional ideas:

1. organic charter law;
2. hierarchy of legal instruments;
3. common-law private-law codification;
4. statutory enactments;
5. regulations, ordinances, and resolutions;
6. agreements of coexistence;
7. resident rights instruments;
8. arbitration and dispute administration;
9. registries and legal-identity systems;
10. special-zone service delivery;
11. legal stability and host-state compliance.

## 5. Próspera Organic-Law Lesson

The Próspera model begins with an organic legal authority granted by Honduran law to ZEDEs. In the Company model, this lesson is translated into a private-law structure:

- the Company requires a supreme internal charter;
- the charter must be subordinate to host-state law;
- any territorial authority must arise from title, lease, concession, contract, license, special-zone authorization, or lawful delegation;
- the Company must maintain a written record explaining the source of its authority in each territory;
- local rules cannot exceed the legal authority granted by the host jurisdiction.

### Company Translation

The Company shall maintain an **Organic Charter Layer** composed of:

1. the Charter Code;
2. Entrenched Articles;
3. host-state compliance clauses;
4. delegation or concession records;
5. Local Charters;
6. continuity plans for repeal, non-renewal, invalidation, or political reversal.

## 6. Próspera Charter Lesson

Próspera's charter functions as a local constitutional document for its internal governance. The Company adopts the same design principle in private form:

- the Charter Code is the supreme internal instrument;
- the Charter Code creates the offices, rights, procedures, rule hierarchy, and limitations of the Company;
- the Charter Code binds the Company as a self-binding commercial constitution;
- subordinate instruments are valid only if consistent with the Charter Code and host-state law.

### Company Translation

The Company shall treat this README and the Charter Code as the index and working text of its private constitutional framework until a separate formal legal instrument is ratified.

## 7. Próspera Rule-Hierarchy Lesson

The Próspera model distinguishes between different classes of legal instruments. The Company shall use the following adapted hierarchy:

1. host-state constitutional, statutory, regulatory, and court authority;
2. mandatory public law of the host jurisdiction;
3. the Company's corporate law and governing instruments;
4. the Charter Code;
5. Entrenched Articles of the Charter Code;
6. ratified Organic Acts;
7. ratified Statutory Acts;
8. Local Charters;
9. Regulations;
10. Ordinances;
11. Resolutions;
12. administrative directives;
13. registry rules;
14. token rules;
15. merchant rules;
16. platform rules;
17. contracts, licenses, and occupancy agreements;
18. policies, manuals, and operating procedures.

Host-state law overrides this entire hierarchy in all cases.

## 8. Company Instrument Classes

The Company may use the following instrument classes:

### 8.1 — Charter Code

The Charter Code is the supreme internal legal instrument of the Company. It defines the nature of the Company, the Governor's authority, the non-sovereignty rule, the status of persons, core rights, tokenized licenses, dispute resolution, compliance, and amendment procedures.

### 8.2 — Organic Acts

Organic Acts implement structural features of the Company, including offices, succession, local charters, registries, courts or tribunals, compliance offices, ombudsman procedures, and relationships with host governments.

### 8.3 — Statutory Acts

Statutory Acts provide generally applicable rules for specific subject areas, including labor, merchants, arbitration, tokens, treasury, data protection, infrastructure, environmental standards, security, access control, and residency.

### 8.4 — Regulations

Regulations provide detail on how Charter provisions and Acts are administered and enforced.

### 8.5 — Ordinances

Ordinances are temporary operating rules used for urgent service delivery, emergency coordination, market organization, infrastructure transition, or temporary local administration. Unless renewed, an Ordinance should expire after a defined period.

### 8.6 — Resolutions

Resolutions record internal decisions, appointments, delegations, authorizations, registry actions, fee schedules, policy interpretations, and administrative measures.

### 8.7 — Contracts and Agreements

Contracts, licenses, occupancy agreements, merchant agreements, resident agreements, platform terms, and Agreements of Coexistence bind the parties who voluntarily accept them. They may grant greater protections than the Charter Code but may not reduce Entrenched Articles.

## 9. Roatán Common Law Code Lesson

Próspera uses the Roatán Common Law Code as a common-law-inspired private-law foundation. The Company adopts this as a drafting model, not as automatically applicable law.

The Company may create a **Company Common Law Reception Act** to receive, adapt, or restate common-law principles for private use, including:

1. contracts;
2. torts;
3. property and licenses;
4. trusts;
5. agency;
6. fiduciary duties;
7. commercial entities;
8. secured transactions;
9. restitution;
10. private nuisance;
11. remedies;
12. civil procedure for private tribunals and arbitration.

### Company Common-Law First Principles

Any Company common-law code should be based on the following principles:

1. competent adults are self-responsible persons with freedom of action and property use, subject to the equal rights of others;
2. legal entities may define internal voting, fiduciary, officer, director, and ownership rules through written instruments, subject to host law;
3. courts, tribunals, and arbitrators may apply precedent only where logically consistent with written Company law;
4. new legal principles require adoption by Charter amendment, Act, Regulation, or lawful adjudication within the allowed scope of host-state law;
5. no common-law principle may be used to create sovereignty, public taxation, criminal jurisdiction, forced residency, or democratic voting rights inside the Company.

Próspera uses statutes to govern subject areas such as labor and arbitration. The Company adopts the same method in private form.

The Company shall create subject-specific Acts rather than placing all rules directly into the Charter Code. The Charter Code should define structure and rights; Acts should handle operational detail.

## 11. Agreements of Coexistence

Próspera uses Agreements of Coexistence to formalize consent to its charter and governance system. The Company adapts this as a private contract model.

Every Resident, Villager, Chartered Merchant, Contractor, platform user, or territorial participant may be required to accept a written agreement stating:

1. the person's status;
2. the services provided by the Company;
3. the rules accepted by the person;
4. the dispute-resolution process;
5. the fee, rent, dues, or service-charge schedule;
6. the host-state law override;

9. the limits of tokens and licenses;

11. the procedure for suspension, termination, renewal, appeal, and complaint;

## 12. Arbitration and Dispute Resolution

The Próspera model places heavy emphasis on arbitration. The Company adopts arbitration as the preferred method for private civil and commercial disputes.

Arbitration may apply to:

1. contract disputes;
2. merchant disputes;
3. tokenized-license disputes;
4. occupancy disputes where lawful;
5. platform disputes;
6. registry disputes;
7. fee disputes;
8. service-level disputes;
9. internal administrative appeals;
10. disputes between Residents, Villagers, merchants, contractors, users, and the Company.

Arbitration shall not be used to avoid mandatory host-state court jurisdiction, criminal law, consumer-protection law, labor law, tenancy law, securities law, financial regulation, public-law obligations, or non-waivable rights.

## 13. Próspera-Informed Compliance Rule

The Company shall never copy a Próspera rule into operational use merely because it appears useful.

Every imported concept must pass the following test:

1. Is it compatible with the host jurisdiction?
2. Is it private and contractual rather than sovereign?
3. Is it consistent with the Charter Code?
4. Does it preserve the Entrenched Articles?
5. Does it require external counsel?
6. Does it affect securities, payments, custody, labor, housing, land, policing, health, environmental law, or public administration?
7. Does it require host-government authorization?
8. Does it need to be drafted as an Act, Regulation, Local Charter, contract, or policy?

# BOOK I — FOUNDATIONAL PRINCIPLES

## Article 1 — Nature of the Company

The Company is a private legal entity engaged in:

1. trade and logistics;
2. digital infrastructure;
3. lawful crypto and digital-asset services;
4. private registry services;
5. arbitration and dispute-administration support;
6. territorial management services;
7. infrastructure development;
8. community governance administration;
9. compliance, identity, and resident-services platforms;
10. research-zone administration;
11. port, warehouse, market, and freeport-style services where lawful.

The Company may operate physical enclaves, digital jurisdictions, commercial platforms, research zones, private communities, ports, warehouses, marketplaces, or governance-service territories only under lawful title, lease, contract, concession, license, or delegation.

The Company shall remain privately held unless the Governor expressly authorizes otherwise through a formal amendment to this Code.

## Article 2 — Non-Sovereignty Clause

The Company is not a state.

The Company shall not claim:

1. independent diplomatic recognition;
2. national sovereignty;
3. criminal jurisdiction beyond what is delegated by law;
4. authority to wage war;
5. authority to imprison or detain persons beyond what host-state law expressly permits;
6. authority to collect taxes except lawful service fees, assessments, rents, dues, or delegated public charges;
7. authority to exclude lawful public authorities from exercising their jurisdiction;
8. authority to extinguish public rights or mandatory legal protections.

Any territorial governance performed by the Company shall be framed as governance services, administrative management, private-community regulation, or delegated special-zone administration, not sovereign rule.

**Interpretive rule.** Where any provision of this Code is capable of two readings, the reading consistent with this Article prevails.

## Article 3 — Rule of Law

All Company territories and digital communities shall be governed by written rules.

No person may be punished, expelled, fined, suspended, or deprived of access except under published rules or emergency procedures authorized by this Code.

Emergency powers shall be reviewable under Article 13.

Residents, users, merchants, contractors, and Villagers shall have access to a dispute-resolution process meeting the requirements of Book VII.

## Article 3A — Hierarchy of Law

Host-state law overrides this Code in all cases. This Code operates only within the space that applicable law leaves to private ordering.

This Code overrides all subordinate Company instruments, including local charters, platform rules, merchant regulations, token rules, treasury rules, registry rules, and administrative directives.

A contract between the Company and a Resident, Villager, Chartered Merchant, or client may grant rights greater than this Code provides, but may not reduce the protections of the Entrenched Articles.

## Article 3B — Próspera-Informed Interpretation

Where this Code refers to Próspera, ZEDEs, special jurisdictions, common-law codes, arbitration statutes, resident agreements, or organic law, such references are interpretive and comparative only.

They do not incorporate Honduran law into the Company unless expressly adopted by a ratified Company Act and valid under host-state law.

# BOOK II — STATUS, MEMBERSHIP, RESIDENCY, AND TOKENIZED PROPERTY LICENSES

## Article 4 — Classes of Persons

The Company may recognize the following classes:

**Governor** — the supreme executive and interpretive authority of the Company and this Code.

**Legal Representative** — the legally registered representative with binding signatory authority.

**Director** — a senior officer appointed to administer a department or territory.

**Territorial Administrator** — an officer appointed to administer a Company territory, project, enclave, district, facility, or platform.

**Chartered Merchant** — a business or individual authorized to trade under Company rules.

**Resident** — a person lawfully permitted to live within a Company-administered territory.

**Villager** — a member of the Company's digital or physical community platform who is not a Resident.

**Visitor** — a temporary guest with limited access rights.

**Contractor** — a person or entity providing services under contract.

**Credentialed Observer** — a journalist, researcher, auditor, or monitor granted defined transparency access under Article 4A.

**Third Party** — any person interacting with the Company without membership, residency, or contractual status. Former designation "External Citizen" is abolished.

## Article 4A — Credentialed Observers

The Company may credential Observers for defined purposes, durations, and areas.

Observer credentials shall state in writing: the access granted, the access excluded, any confidentiality obligations, and the revocation procedure.

Observer access may be conditioned on safety, security, and privacy rules, but credentials shall not be revoked solely for lawful unfavorable reporting. Revocation for cause is subject to written reasons and Article 20 review.

## Article 5 — Digital-to-Physical Parity

Digital identity shall serve as the primary administrative identity within Company systems.

Physical access to Company-administered territory may be tied to digital status, subject to the limits in this Article.

Suspension of digital access may trigger restriction of physical access to commercial, platform, and non-essential facilities only.

**Dwelling and Essential-Services Guarantee (Entrenched).** No suspension, emergency directive, fee dispute, rule violation, or other Company action shall interrupt, condition, or deny:

1. a Resident's access to their dwelling;
2. essential utilities serving that dwelling, including power, water, sanitation, and any utility legally required;
3. lawful means of entry to and exit from the territory.

Removal of a Resident from a dwelling may occur only through the legal process of the host jurisdiction. This paragraph overrides every other provision of this Code.

Emergency digital suspension may occur immediately where there is a credible threat to safety, systems, infrastructure, property, legal compliance, or public order, subject to Article 13 review and to paragraph 4 of this Article.

## Article 5A — Company Property and Tokenized Area Licenses

**Governor's correction integrated.** The Company owns all property held within the Company's jurisdiction and grants only a license to an area through a token.

### 5A.1 — Company Ownership of Territorial Property

All land, buildings, infrastructure, utilities, common areas, access systems, registries, administrative platforms, and territorial assets held within a Company-administered jurisdiction are owned by the Company or by a Company-controlled holding entity unless a written instrument approved by the Governor expressly provides otherwise.

### 5A.2 — No Private Real-Property Ownership by Token

No Resident, Villager, Chartered Merchant, Visitor, Contractor, user, or Third Party acquires ownership of Company territorial property merely by holding a token, occupying an area, paying fees, using services, participating in a platform, or remaining in possession for any period of time.

### 5A.3 — Token as License Credential

A token, digital credential, smart-contract entry, registry entry, access pass, or similar instrument issued by the Company represents a contractual license to use, occupy, access, trade from, administer, or receive services connected to a defined area or entitlement. The token does not transfer title to the underlying property.

### 5A.4 — Token Does Not Confer Sovereign or Governance Rights

A tokenized license is not:

1. a deed;
2. a freehold estate;
3. a fee simple estate;
4. a sovereign grant;
5. a citizenship instrument;
6. a voting right;
7. a referendum right;
8. a share of the Company;
9. a right to govern the Company;
10. a right to exclude public authorities acting under host-state law;
11. a tax instrument;
12. a security, unless applicable law determines otherwise.

### 5A.5 — License Agreement Required

Every tokenized area license shall be governed by a written license agreement, occupancy agreement, merchant agreement, platform agreement, Local Charter provision, or other written Company instrument stating:

1. the area, unit, facility, or entitlement covered;
2. the permitted use;
3. the duration of the license;
4. the fees, rents, dues, or charges owed;
5. renewal terms;
6. transferability or non-transferability;
7. grounds for suspension, termination, or non-renewal;
8. dispute process;
9. relationship to host-state law;
10. whether the license may be assigned, inherited, pledged, sold, revoked, renewed, or restricted.

### 5A.6 — Host-State Law Override

If host-state law treats a tokenized license, occupancy agreement, merchant agreement, access credential, or dwelling arrangement as a lease, tenancy, concession, consumer contract, security, payment instrument, regulated financial product, land interest, or other regulated legal relationship, host-state law controls.

The Company shall structure and administer tokenized licenses only within the limits of applicable law.

### 5A.7 — Private Movable Property Protected

Company ownership of territorial land and infrastructure does not extend to the lawful personal property, tools, inventory, goods, documents, clothing, devices, or private effects of Residents, Villagers, Chartered Merchants, Visitors, Contractors, users, or Third Parties.

### 5A.8 — Registry Supremacy Within Company Systems Only

The Company registry shall be the authoritative internal record of tokenized licenses, but it shall not override host-state land registries, corporate registries, court orders, public records, or mandatory legal filings.

## Article 6 — Voluntary Association

No person shall be compelled to become a Resident, Villager, Chartered Merchant, or user of Company services.

All membership shall be voluntary and contractual.

**Absolute Exit Right (Entrenched).** Every person has the unconditional right to physically depart any Company-administered territory at any time. Departure shall never be conditioned on payment of debts, completion of procedures, surrender of property lawfully owned by the departing person, or Company consent. Outstanding debts and contractual obligations survive departure as ordinary civil claims, enforceable only through lawful process.

Safety procedures, such as checkpoint screening on exit from hazardous or restricted facilities, may regulate the manner of departure but never its availability.

# BOOK III — GOVERNING AUTHORITIES

## Article 7 — The Governor

The Governor is the supreme internal authority of the Company. Nothing in this Code transfers final control of the Company from the Governor.

The Governor may adopt, amend, repeal, suspend, interpret, or ratify:

1. this Code, subject to Entrenched Articles;
2. operating bylaws;
3. Organic Acts;
4. Statutory Acts;
5. Regulations;
6. Ordinances;
7. Resolutions;
8. Local Charters;
9. digital-platform rules;
10. merchant regulations;
11. treasury rules;
12. token rules;
13. arbitration rules;
14. emergency directives;
15. compliance policies.

The Governor's authority is internal and corporate. It does not override host-state law.

The Governor may delegate authority in writing but may not permanently surrender final interpretive authority unless the Company is formally reorganized.

**Self-binding principle.** Constraints in this Code on the manner and timing of the Governor's powers exist to make the Company's commitments credible to residents, clients, and host governments. They limit how power is exercised, not who holds it.

## Article 7A — Succession and Incapacity

The Governor shall maintain a written, dated instrument of succession designating one or more successors in order of priority, lodged with the Legal Representative and the Office of Compliance and Legality.

Upon the Governor's death, legal incapacity, or written resignation, the designated successor assumes the office of Governor with full authority under this Code.

If no valid succession instrument exists, the Legal Representative shall serve as Acting Governor with all powers except amendment of the Entrenched Articles, and shall convene the Court of Directors within 30 days to identify a permanent Governor in accordance with the Company's corporate constitution and applicable corporate law.

A dispute over succession shall be resolved under the corporate law of the Company's jurisdiction of incorporation. During any such dispute, no party may amend this Code, move the treasury beyond ordinary operations, or alter resident rights.

## Article 7B — Proprietary Governance; Prohibition of Electoral Mechanisms (Entrenched)

**Non-democratic model declared.** The Company is governed proprietarily, not democratically. Authority within the Company flows from ownership, appointment, and contract — never from election, vote, referendum, or majority will of Residents, Villagers, Chartered Merchants, users, or any other class of persons.

Within Company governance and within every Company-administered territory, platform, and instrument:

1. no Company office is or may be made elective;
2. no class of persons holds, or may acquire by any means, voting rights, electoral rights, initiative rights, recall rights, or referendum rights over the Company, this Code, any Local Charter, any rule, any fee, or any officer;
3. no Local Charter, platform rule, merchant regulation, token design, smart contract, or delegated instrument may introduce, simulate, or evolve toward an electoral, plebiscitary, or majority-rule governance mechanism;
4. no petition, poll, survey, assembly, comment process, or governance-comment instrument creates binding effect;
5. tokens shall never confer governance votes, and any token, credential, or contract term purporting to do so is void from inception.

This Article governs the Company's own constitution and instruments only. It does not restrict any person's civic or political rights under host-state law.

## Article 8 — The Court of Directors

The Company may maintain a Court of Directors.

The Court of Directors advises the Governor on:

1. trade;
2. finance;
3. territorial administration;
4. legal compliance;
5. digital assets;
6. infrastructure;
7. security;
8. relations with host governments;
9. resident welfare;
10. dispute systems;
11. Próspera-informed legal architecture and comparative special-jurisdiction models.

The Court of Directors has no power to override the Governor unless expressly granted by this Code.

For any amendment of an Entrenched Article, any token issuance, or any treasury movement above the Material Threshold, the Governor shall obtain and preserve the Court's written advice before acting. The Governor may act against that advice, but the advice and the Governor's written reasons become part of the Company record available to auditors and, where contractually promised, to affected stakeholders.

## Article 9 — Territorial Administrators

Each Company-administered territory may have a Territorial Administrator.

The Administrator is responsible for:

1. local service delivery;
2. registry operations;
3. utilities coordination;
4. local rule enforcement;
5. resident onboarding;
6. emergency management;
7. community relations;
8. reporting to the Governor;
9. maintaining the Local Charter and authority file.

The Administrator acts as an officer of a private governance-services provider, not as a governor of a sovereign territory.

## Article 10 — Office of Compliance and Legality

The Company shall maintain an Office of Compliance and Legality.

This office shall oversee:

1. host-law compliance;
2. anti-money-laundering controls;
3. sanctions compliance;
4. securities-law review;
5. data-protection obligations;
6. consumer protection;
7. tax reporting;
8. labor compliance;
9. financial licensing;
10. environmental compliance;
11. land, lease, housing, concession, and tenancy compliance;
12. public-authority liaison;
13. Próspera-informed comparative law review.

No digital token, treasury system, financial service, or trading program may launch without internal compliance review and written external legal counsel where jurisdictionally required.

The Company shall not operate payment, settlement, custody, exchange, lending, brokerage, investment, or money-transmission services in any jurisdiction without the license or registration that jurisdiction requires, or a written external legal opinion that none is required.

## Article 10A — The Ombudsman

The Company shall retain an independent Ombudsman who is not an employee of the Company and whose compensation is fixed in advance for a defined term.

The Ombudsman may receive complaints from any Resident, Villager, Chartered Merchant, Contractor, user, or Credentialed Observer; investigate with access to relevant Company records; and issue written, non-binding recommendations to the Governor.

The Governor retains full authority to accept or reject any recommendation, but rejections of recommendations concerning Entrenched Articles shall be in writing and preserved in the Company record.

No person shall be penalized for raising a complaint with the Ombudsman in good faith.

# BOOK IV — RIGHTS AND DUTIES

## Article 11 — Company Bill of Rights (Entrenched)

Every Resident, Villager, Chartered Merchant, Contractor, and lawful user shall have the following internal rights:

1. right to written notice of binding rules;
2. right to contractual clarity;
3. right to due process before non-emergency expulsion;
4. right to appeal suspension or expulsion;
5. right to protection of lawful private movable property, deposits, refunds, contractual entitlements, and tokenized license interests, subject to Company ownership of territorial property under Article 5A;
6. right to privacy and data protection;
7. right to fair access to dispute resolution, including independent arbitration under Article 20;
8. right to leave the community under Article 6;
9. right to non-discrimination under applicable law;
10. right to be free from forced labor, coercion, unlawful detention, or physical abuse;
11. right of Residents to dwelling and essential services under Article 5;
12. right to receive wages, refunds, and deposit returns in lawful money of the host state, never solely in internal Company units;
13. right to complain to the Ombudsman;
14. right to have host-state law recognized as superior to Company law.

## Article 12 — Duties of Members

Every Resident, Villager, Chartered Merchant, Contractor, and user shall:

1. obey applicable host-state law;
2. obey Company rules;
3. respect the rights of others;
4. avoid damaging Company infrastructure;
5. avoid malware, unlawful data, fraud, theft, harassment, or unauthorized access;
6. comply with identity, safety, and financial rules;
7. pay lawful fees, rents, charges, or dues owed under contract;
8. cooperate with lawful investigations by the Company or public authorities;
9. preserve safety, utilities, common areas, and essential infrastructure.

## Article 13 — Due Process

No person shall be permanently expelled from a Company-administered territory without:

1. written notice;
2. stated reasons;
3. opportunity to respond;
4. review by a designated officer, tribunal, or independent arbitrator as Article 20 requires.

Emergency suspension may occur first where delay creates a serious risk.

Every emergency suspension shall be reviewed:

1. within 72 hours, where the suspension affects residency privileges, dwelling-adjacent services, or a person's primary livelihood within the territory;
2. within 14 days, for platform-only, merchant-privilege, or facility-access suspensions.

A suspension not reviewed within the applicable period lapses automatically.

Removal of a Resident from a dwelling remains governed exclusively by Article 5 and host-state law.

# BOOK V — TERRITORIAL GOVERNANCE SERVICES

# BOOK VII — COMMON LAW, ARBITRATION, AND DISPUTE ADMINISTRATION

## Article 19 — Company Common Law Reception

The Company may adopt a Company Common Law Reception Act to provide default private-law rules for contracts, torts, property licenses, trusts, agency, fiduciary duties, remedies, and commercial relations.

The Company common-law layer shall be used as a gap-filling and interpretive tool only within the scope permitted by contract and applicable law.

## Article 20 — Arbitration and Dispute Administration

The Company may require private arbitration for disputes arising under Company contracts, licenses, merchant rules, platform terms, Local Charters, and Agreements of Coexistence, to the extent permitted by law.

Arbitration rules shall provide:

2. notice;
3. opportunity to be heard;
4. evidence procedures;
5. emergency relief where necessary;
6. written decision;
7. review or appeal where required by Company rule or host-state law;
8. enforceability under applicable arbitration law;
9. protection for non-waivable rights;
10. clear relationship to courts of the host jurisdiction.

The Company shall not use arbitration to evade criminal law, public-law duties, non-waivable labor law, non-waivable consumer law, housing protections, securities law, financial regulation, environmental law, or mandatory court jurisdiction.

## Article 21 — Administrative Review

Administrative decisions by Company officers may be reviewed through internal appeal, Ombudsman complaint, independent review officer, or arbitration as applicable.

No administrative review process may deny a person the right to seek relief from a host-state court where such relief cannot lawfully be waived.

# PART VI — MASTER INTERPRETIVE PRINCIPLES

## 1. Host-State Supremacy

The Law Library operates only within the legal space that host-state law leaves to private ordering.

## 2. Private Governance Services

All governance performed by the Company is governance as a service, administrative management, private-community regulation, territorial management, platform administration, or delegated special-zone administration.

## 3. No Sovereign Rule

No Company rule may be interpreted as a claim to independent sovereignty, statehood, diplomatic power, war power, independent criminal jurisdiction, taxation, or exclusion of lawful public authorities.

## 4. Company Ownership of Territory

The Company owns the territorial property within its jurisdiction. Persons inside the Company's systems may receive tokenized licenses to defined areas, but they do not receive ownership of the underlying Company property unless a separate lawful written instrument expressly provides otherwise.

## 5. Tokens Are Licenses, Not Government

Tokens may represent access, occupancy, use, trade, registry, service, or contractual entitlements. Tokens do not create votes, elections, citizenship, sovereignty, public office, land title, or governance rights.

## 6. Governor Authority

The Governor remains the supreme internal authority of the Company, subject to host-state law, corporate law, written self-binding rules, and entrenched protections contained in the Charter Code.

## 7. Credible Self-Binding

The Company binds itself to written rules because durable commerce requires credible promises, predictable administration, lawful conduct, and protection of residents, merchants, users, clients, and host-government partners.

## 8. Próspera as Study Model Only

Próspera's legal architecture is studied as a useful model of organic law, common-law reception, statutory enactments, arbitration, registries, and agreements of coexistence. It does not grant authority to the Company unless independently authorized by applicable law.

## 9. Agent Coordination Rule

All project agents shall treat this README as the canonical law-library surface unless the Governor identifies another controlling instrument. Agents may propose improvements, but they shall not silently remove host-state supremacy, non-sovereignty, proprietary governance, token-license limits, compliance review, resident protections, or the Próspera-informed legal architecture.
